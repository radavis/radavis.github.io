## Overview of Clinical Trials

Clinical Trials are performed for both drugs and devices. Trials exist to determine
the safety and effectiveness of these new products. Testing starts in the lab.
Researchers must apply to the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to proceed with
their study the of new product on humans. Assuming the application process requires
some level of scientific evidence that the drug or device works in the lab.

Human Clinical Trials can proceed once approval has been obtained by the FDA.
This is typically conducted in phases.

* Phase I: Safety
  - Answer the question, "Is the new product safe?"
  - Trial phase lasts several months.
  - Involves 20 to 100 paid volunteers.
  - What are the effects of the product on humans?
  - How is the drug absorbed, metabolized, and excreted?
  - What side effects are observed?
  - ~70% of new drugs pass this phase.
* Phase II: Efficacy
  - Answer the question, "Is the new product effective?"
  - Trial phase lasts several months to two years.
  - Involves hundreds of patients in a randomized, double-blind experiment.
  - ~33% of new drugs pass Phase I and II.
* Phase III: Scale
  - Gain a more thorough understanding of the effectiveness of the new product.
  - Determine the range of adverse reactions to the new product.
  - Trial phase can last several years.
  - Can involve thousands of patients.
  - ~80% of new drugs that enter this phase pass.
  - Once complete, permission to market the drug can be requested from the FDA.
* Phase IV: Post-Marketing Surveillance Trials
  - Compare the new product to others already on the market.
  - Monitor the product's long-term effectiveness and impact on patient quality of life.
  - Determine the cost effectiveness compared to existing and new treatments.


## Clinical Trial Management System (CTMS)

Software used by biotech and pharmaceutical industries to manage clinical trials
in clinical research. The system maintains and manages planning, performing and
reporting functions, along with participant contact information, deadlines,
and milestones.

* eClinical - trial automation technology that encompasses the entire business process of managing clinical trials. This may include:
  - Electronic Data Capture (EDC) - data entry, validation, and reporting [[wiki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_data_capture)]
  - Interactive Voice Response Systems (IVR) - accepts voice and DTMF tones
  - Interactive Web Response (IWR)
  - Randomization and Trial Supply Management (RTSM)
  - Electronic Patient Diaries


## Goals of a CTMS

* Streamline Workflows
* Eliminate duplication of data and entry effort.
* Provide accurate and up-to-date information on the Clinical Trial.


## Functions of a CTMS

* budgeting
* patient management and recruitment
* compliance with government regulations
* EDC
* adverse event reporting


## Locations where a CTMS may be used

Anywhere clinical research is conducted.

* research hospitals
* physician practices
* academic medical centers
* cancer centers
