# Pair Programming Guidelines

Goal: Form a set of guidelines when working collaboratively.


## Why is this important?

Everyone has a different working style. When working closely with someone else,
it is important to understand how your partner works best: Do they need long
stretches of time to ponder a problem? Do they prefer to think "out loud?"
Understanding these differences in individual working style can help us shift our
collaborative working style to accommodate our pair, and to discover a way to
work best together and achieve shared goals. If you are more extroverted than
your pair, be sure to give your pair space to think: pauses in communication for
deep thought are normal. If you are more introverted, remember to regularly
share what you are thinking.


## Step 0: Is this problem ready for Pair Programming?

State reasons why you might choose to not pair on this particular problem.
Perhaps a different approach is warranted, especially if pairing has not yielded
forward progress, or if neither party has knowledge on the technology necessary
to solve the problem. In the former case, consider bringing in one or more
developers to discuss the problem to discover a way forward. In the latter case,
consider using solo development time to research and to work through tutorials to
get up-to-speed in that specific technology.


## First, Define the Goal

Clearly define the goal or task you and your pair will collaborate in order to
solve. Often, you will have a list of tasks defined as stories ready for you to
pick up in your project management app (e.g. - Jira, Tracker). Pick a story,
read it out loud, and talk about it. Is it clearly defined? Can it be broken up
into a set of smaller, easier tasks? What are the edge cases? Ensure both you
and your pair have a clear understanding of the problem. Then, formulate an
agreed upon approach to solving the problem.


## Block out distractions

Turn off notifications. Silence your phone. You and your pair have decided to
dedicate time to each other to solve a shared problem. Do your best to eliminate
any distractions which could keep you from being productive. If someone else
approaches you with a question, politely state that you are busy at the moment,
and suggest a time in the near future in which you could talk: "I'm sorry,
Jen and I are in the middle of a difficult problem at the moment. Will you be
available to chat in 30 minutes?"


## Take regular breaks

Taking a break in the middle of working collaboratively might seem
counterintuitive. However, taking regular breaks is necessary to maintain your
attention span and to maximize your productivity. Every 50-90 minutes, take a
15-20 minute break that involves something other than looking at a screen.
Take a walk. Talk with a co-worker. Most importantly, take a mental break from
the problem at hand. Watch for indicators that indicate a break is necessary.
Does you or you pair feel distracted? Have you been working for more than 50
minutes? Announce that a break is needed and specify a time to meet back up.
"Hey, I need a break. Let's meet back up in 20 minutes at 11:05."
