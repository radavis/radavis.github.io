As a web developers, we are constantly learning. It feels like as soon as you
have a handle on some new shiny library, something "better" comes along and
makes your favorite shiny new library "obsolete". That library was "the old way
of thinking!" This new thing is better!

Rinse and repeat.

I do enjoy learning new things, but there's also the following struggle to
contend with: Delivering software on-time, within budget, that meets the
end-user's needs, with as few bugs as possible. If I'm learning the new thing
while building the solution to my user's problems, how can I ensure I'm
balancing business needs with my need to maintain my skills and knowledge in a
changing ecosystem?

## Fall back to core standards

One strategy that I use is to fall back on the technologies that aren't going
anywhere. When presented with a design, I'm going to implement it using plain
old HTML and CSS, first. Then, I convert that implementation to whatever
whizbang frontend framework du jour my team is currently using. The benefit of
approaching the problem this way are that I get the opportunity to practice
technologies that will withstand the test of time. If JSS is the new hotness,
how am I going to use it effectively if I don't understand how CSS works?
Likewise, If I'm using a component-based UI library, how am I going to use it
effectively if I don't know the rules of HTML?

You might be saying, "But Richard, isn't this the opposite of moving fast and
breaking things?" And, I'd say, "Exactly."

We have an obsession with doing things faster. Fast doesn't necessarily mean
good. Sure, it's great if you deliver something quickly. But, you also want to
deliver something of quality. Remember that saying, "Good, fast, cheap. Pick two."
"Good" is the only quality that's going to keep clients coming back to you. So,
take your time and do it right.

## Ask for an explanation

There's plenty of times when reviewing code, and I say to myself, "What the heck
is happening, here?" I'll spend some time teasing apart a pull request, and
attempt to wrap my mind around the implementation. Ultimately, if I'm stuck
trying to understand some code, I'll ask for the author to explain their code.
Often times, it's helpful to get everyone around the pull request so that
knowledge can be shared.

I've been on the flip-side of this, too. I'll reach out to my teammates and ask
for a code review. They they might look at it and say, "I don't know what's
going on here. Hopefully, someone else can give Richard a proper code review."

It might be taboo to some developers to admit you "just don't get it." If your
team or company culture shames people for not understanding some bit of code,
or not "knowing" a library or framework, I feel for you. You can try to employ
a culture shift by having a one-on-one conversation with individuals who
practice knowledge shaming. Express that their behavior is damaging to team
morale and the company culture. If this behavior is deeply rooted in the
organization, it might not be possible to change. Best to spruce up your resume
and start looking for better co-workers.

## Reference high-quality examples

Know where to find these in your codebase, or in other projects. Seeing how
a feature comes together is helpful. You can reference them when you need to
build something similar, or point to them when on-boarding new team members so
they know how to get up-to-speed quickly with contributing to the project.
