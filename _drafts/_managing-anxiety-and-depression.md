# Managing Anxiety and Depression

These past few months, my mind has been overcome with negative thoughts. 
The progression goes something like this...

* I make a singular mistake, or I am unable to get my point across to another 
  person.
* The negative loop starts. I think, "How could I have fouled up like this?"
* My mind replays the event. I look at the scenario, over and over again, from 
  every possible perspective.
* I think of how I could have acted differently in this past moment to affect a 
  different outcome.
* I feel upset with myself for not "being good enough."
* This negative feeling stirs up other past events that have impacted me in a 
  negative way.
* I'm now on the rollercoaster of shitty emotions, and there's no getting off.
* Imposter Syndrome shows up, wielding it's Hammer of Doubt, and strikes me 
  square in the frontal cortex.
* All my past accompishments are questioned, belittled, or forgotten in the haze
  of my negative mindset.
* I convince myself that I am incapable of doing anything right.

At this point in the timeline, I'm in a state of complete hopelessness. I've 
allowed my primitive mind to look for any and all threats to myself, and it has 
done an excellent job of hightening my anxiety, highlighting all that I've ever 
done wrong, and dredging up all of the worst events in my mind's stockpile. 

My "Monkey Mind," the ever-present voice looking for threats from my environment,
has tricked me into walking down this trail of negative thinking. To borrow 
Martin Seligman's terminology from _Learned Optimism_, my negative thoughts have
become personal, pervasive, and permanent.

## Quieting your Monkey Mind

In [this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4PkrhH-bkpk), 
Tibetan Buddhhist Master Mingyur Rinpoche, explains how to quell the chatter of 
your primitive mind. Your brain has evolved to look for threats everywhere. 
Consider that a check in the 'plus' column for you and your genetic material 
being in existence, today. Unfortunately, this need to look for danger in the 
everyday is no longer helful to everyone's survival. Left unchecked, your 
Monkey Mind will chatter at you, unearthing all the perceived threats, both valid
and invalid, it can conjure up. 

The simple breathing technique described by Mingur Rinpoche can silence this 
unwanted noise, increase your ability to concentrate on the present, and 
ultimately help to bring your mind back to a place of positive thought. Practice 
it regularly. Focus your conscious thought on breathing slowly whenever you feel 
anxious and overwhelmed. This exercise will help you to calm your thoughts, 
clear your mind, and direct your emotions to a more positive place.

## Write your Morning Pages

I was introduced to the concept of **Morning Pages** through Andy Hunt's book
_Pragmatic Thinking and Learning_. The exercise is quite simple...

* Wake up.
* Write three pages of your conscious thought, on paper, by hand.

The idea is that when you first wake up, you are still groggy from last night's
rest. Your ability to self-edit and critique is compromised. You're closer to
your subconscious mind, and able to tap into it. 

I've found this exercise to be quite helpful for quelling my anxities. I started
writing Morning Pages before I moved from Philadelphia to Boston. I was moving
there to start my time at Launch Academy. I had put in my notice at work. I was
moving to a completely new city, to learn Software Development. My anxiety was 
at its peak: What if I fail? What if I fuck up? What if no one likes me? What if
I'm not good enough?

The interesting thing I've discovered about Morning Pages is that they don't 
have to be written in the morning, and they don't have to be three pages long. 
The simple process of getting your thoughs out of your mind onto paper is 
medication for the mind. Expresing your thoughts in your own words in a 
completely safe environment is how you heal.

Another way to externalize your thoughts can help manage your anxiety.

## Externalize your Thoughts Through Conversation

Share you thoughts and feelings with friends whom you trust.

By externalizing your thoughts, through conversation, and through writing, you
are engaging two of your earliest learned behaviors.

## Indulge in passtimes you enjoy

Reading, watching movies, playing videogames, crafting, home repair. Use your
hobbies as an outlet. The key here is to do something you are good at and that 
you enjoy.

## Play Tetris

I sometimes joke that wherever I go, I should carry around a 1989 GameBoy with 
a copy of Tetris plugged into the back, because life is a traumatic event.

When your thoughts stray to the negative, focusing your concious energy on 
putting things in order gives you some control over your environment.

I think completing a puzzle falls into this realm, as well. The simple act of 
taking something that is in pieces, and making it complete, has a healing effect.

## Complete Simple Tasks

Identify small tasks you feel up to attempting. Doing the dishes by hand is one
of my favorites. It has a start and an end. You can take a break if you need one.

Clean the bathroom. Sweep the floor. Wash a load of laundry. Clean your room.

These simple tasks count towards making your world a little more positive. 
Which, in turn, make you feel slightly better. The key in all of this is viewing
the world in a more positive light. Sure, bad things happen to people. Sure,
life is fucking pointles. But, it's all we've got.
